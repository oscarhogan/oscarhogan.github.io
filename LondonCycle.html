How can London become Europe's greatest large cycling city?

The benefits of cycling are well established. Both on the individual; Disease risk reduction and higher life expectancy - as well as on society; reduced traffic fatilites and lower ambient air pollution. Many European cities use segregated cycleway infrastructure to improve the perception of safety around cycling, which is the single biggest impedence to uptake.

While London has made strides in creating a less hostile environment for cyclists, studies mark a divide between those that choose to cycle and those that do not. Demographic differences in tendency to cycle reflect an environment where cycleway planning is often built upon existing usage patterns.

How then, does the consideration of a range of needs and desires in the cycleway planning process inform network planning? And how can geospatial data analysis be used to help create an active travel environment that best reflects the needs of London's diverse population, achieving the cities aspiration of becoming a leading cycle city.

Multi-Criteria Evaluation

Understanding where cycleway investment is most prudent neccesitates an understanding of how and why people cycle, and the barriers they face. While not all-encompasing, Multi-Criteria Evaluation (MCE) provides a framework to consider the role of different factors

MCE weighs different input criteria on the basis of importance, assigning geographic areas a total value. In the case of cycleway planning, looking for locations which best satisify the demands of a diverse potential userbase, informs an inclusive network.

An adjusted version of the Bicycle Levels of Service (BLoS) which is used to assess the quality of existing infrastructure was developed to reflect the needs of user groups, underepresented among cyclists who may hold differing route priorities. For example older adults may value routes with lower maximum gradients whereas female cyclists may prioritise street lighting and visibility.
